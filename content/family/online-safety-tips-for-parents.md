# Online Safety Tips for Parents

Online Safety Tips for Parents start with your own habits. Kids copy what you do, so calm guidance—rather than fear—helps children and teens follow safety rules. Use these steps to model safe behavior, stay informed about new apps, and keep conversations open.

## Why this is a risk

- Kids model what they see—unsafe parent habits weaken house rules.  
- New apps and scams change quickly; outdated advice can miss real risks.  
- Over-monitoring can push kids to hide their online activity.

## What you should do

- Model basics: unique passwords, a password manager, 2FA on key accounts, screen locks, and careful link-checking. Let kids see you doing it.  
- Set clear, age-based rules for sharing, chatting, downloads, and time limits; write them down and post them near shared devices.  
- Stay curious: ask kids to demo their favorite apps; learn privacy settings together and let them show you new features.  
- Keep regular “digital check-ins” (weekly for young kids, biweekly for teens) to review new apps, privacy settings, and any uncomfortable messages.  
- Build a “pause and ask” family habit: anytime a pop-up, new link, or request for personal info appears, everyone pauses and checks.  
- Separate profiles: use child or teen accounts with restricted installs; keep your admin account private and PIN-protected.  
- Know recovery paths: store backup codes for email/cloud accounts; practice account recovery so you can act fast after a lockout.

<Info>Make safety a conversation, not a surprise inspection. Predictable check-ins build trust and openness.</Info>

## Build a family safety plan

- Define 3–5 house rules (sharing, downloads, screen time, location sharing, reporting weird stuff).  
- Post the rules visibly; revisit them monthly and adjust as kids mature.  
- Agree on “who to tell” for different issues: scams, bullying, unwanted chats, or suspicious downloads.  
- Decide device locations and hours: common areas for young kids, no unsupervised late-night use for younger teens.  
- Create a simple consequence ladder that restores trust (review rules, practice together, then regain privileges).

## Stay informed without panic

- Subscribe to one trusted source (school tech bulletin, a security newsletter, or platform safety pages) and skim weekly.  
- When kids mention a new app, look up its safety settings together; turn on private profiles, disable location by default, and limit DMs to friends.  
- Keep a small “test device” or secondary profile to explore new apps before approving them for kids.  
- Update devices monthly: browser, OS, and apps—do it as a family mini “update night.”  
- Avoid knee-jerk bans; instead, set conditions: “You can use this app with these privacy settings and check-ins.”

## Model the behavior you expect

- Lock your own screens when you step away; narrate why: “I lock my phone to protect our info.”  
- Show how you check sender addresses and URLs before clicking links; explain red flags aloud.  
- Avoid oversharing your own routines or locations on social media; kids notice.  
- Use respectful online tone; remind kids that kindness and privacy go together.  
- Demonstrate how to decline friend requests or DMs from unknown people and how to report abuse.

## Guide kids and teens by age

- Young kids: co-watch, use whitelisted apps, disable chats, and practice “pause and ask.”  
- Preteens: teach private profiles, friend approvals, and why we avoid random DMs; allow limited search with safe search on.  
- Teens: focus on reputation, privacy, scams, and consent; encourage them to teach you about their apps and set shared agreements.  
- All ages: keep devices in shared spaces during vulnerable hours (late night, before school).

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Using one-size-fits-all rules for different ages.  
- Reacting with anger when kids report mistakes—reward honesty instead.  
- Relying only on parental controls without teaching judgment.

## Recovery playbook (when something goes wrong)

- Stay calm and thank your child for telling you.  
- Collect evidence: screenshots, usernames, links, timestamps.  
- Change passwords and enable 2FA on affected accounts; sign out of all sessions.  
- Remove suspicious apps or extensions; run a security scan if available.  
- Report and block the offending account or app content.  
- If money or personal data is involved, contact your bank/school quickly and monitor statements/alerts.  
- Debrief later: what worked, what to change in rules or device setup.

## Summary

Lead by example, stay informed, and keep the dialogue open. Online Safety Tips for Parents work when you model strong passwords and 2FA, practice “pause and ask,” keep predictable check-ins, and adjust rules by age. Trust plus clear expectations helps kids come to you early, before small issues become big ones.
